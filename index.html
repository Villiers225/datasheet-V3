Armies & Technology — Data Sheet

1. War’s relationship with technology has changed.

  • The 4IR has seen military hardware become more intelligent, connected and software-defined; and progress continues.
    Software dominance
    Capability hierarchy inverted: software now leads hardware design and upgrade tempo. [9]
  • These smart machines have changed the character of combat (recce-strike; active survival; combat economics).
    Combat economics & transparency
    UAV saturation + sensor fusion collapse manoeuvre surprise; $300k–$2m vehicles neutralised by FPVs costing hundreds. Precision fires attrit mass before contact. [4]
  • Software-defined hardware now drives army competitiveness.
    Edge = upgrade velocity
    Advantage tracks “ability to rapidly update, adapt and deploy software functionality.” OTA-capable stacks compound performance with every drop. [3]
  • Autonomy appears to be a generational inflection point.
    Autonomy leads defence-tech VC
    Largest single category by funding; front lines already shaped by 30-km autonomous/semiautonomous drone attrition belts. [7]

2. NATO’s defence-technology ecosystem has shifted significantly in response.

  • Proliferation of new, smaller, investor-backed specialist vendors.
    Capital reallocation
    VC up ~600% since 2018; European investor count up 3.9× vs 2019. [6]
  • Especially proficient at low-cost, commercially-driven, software-centric, AI-enabled technologies and autonomous systems.
    Cycle-time inversion
    Software-first vendors iterate in weeks; primes’ waterfall cycles run for years. Rapid deployment concentrates in autonomy, sensing, C2.
  • Tech companies and investors are now major players in defence innovation – but challenges remain (integration, scaling).
    Integration drag
    Closed legacy architectures slow modern software adoption; even F-22 and F-35 cannot natively exchange data. [3]
  • Investors are betting on a market shift from big→small; but many hedge by backing the primes.
    Signal
    Defence is Europe’s fastest-growing VC sector; markets price sustained share transfer to software-centric defence-tech platforms. [6]

3. We have not adequately adjusted to either change.

  • Most NATO armies lack a winning response to disruption created by new recce-strike technologies.
    Heavy forces attrited
    Cheap drones + pervasive ISR degrade armour; forces attrited before contact; mass ≠ survivability. [4]
  • CAPDEV mindsets and models make purchasing novel software-defined hardware difficult, especially from new vendors.
    Waterfall failure
    Requirement-locked programs misfit software cadence; example: F-22 software upgrade planned 5 years, took 12, cost ~2×.
  • Upgrading software in-service and increasing platform performance remains difficult.
    Architecture fragmentation
    Proprietary, non-interoperable stacks block continuous upgrades; data-sharing limits persist across fleets. [3]
  • Europe has not internalised the need for a sovereign defence-technology ecosystem.
    System proliferation
    UK, Germany, Italy import >60% hardware from the US; Germany among least digitalised forces. [2]

4. A new paradigm is required.

  • Place software-defined capability at the centre and normalise rapid upgrades and iteration.
    Horizontal software spine
    Software becomes the scaled layer; hardware slots in. OTA, open interfaces, containerised services shorten test-field loops. [3]
  • Future security and prosperity depend on nurturing NATO’s defence-technology ecosystem.
    Base already formed
    $9.1bn
    ~600% growth since 2018 indicates a viable sovereign Alliance-wide tech base forming. [6]
  • Become a better customer: spend, communicate, support integration and scaling.
    Procurement shift
    Replace cost-plus with agile, iterative contracting; create frontline–developer feedback loops (Ukraine model). [7]
  • Requires shifts in philosophy, processes and funding orthodoxies.
    Strategic warning
    Without structural change NATO faces progressive, irreversible erosion of military edge. [7]
